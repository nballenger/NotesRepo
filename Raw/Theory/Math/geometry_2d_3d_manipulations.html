Trigonometry

  • Posit a triangle with sides a, b, and c, where:
    • ∠ab = 90°
    • ∠ac = Θ°
    • ∠bc = (90 - Θ)°
  • Given those angles, let:
    • a be termed the adjacent (to Θ) side
    • b be termed the opposite (from Θ) side
    • c be termed the hypoteneuse
  • Further, posit a circle on a cartesian plane, centered at the origin and with a radius of 1. Call this the unit circle.
  • For any given point on a unit circle, a right triangle may be created where the hypoteneuse is the radius of the unit circle.
  • There exist six possible ratios of the length of the sides of a right triangle:
    Name ƒ Ratio WRT a Unit Circle
    Sine sin Θ opphyp x
    Cosine cos Θ adjhyp y
    Tangent tan Θ oppadj sinΘcosΘ
    Cosecant csc Θ hypopp 1sinΘ
    Secant sec Θ hypadj 1cosΘ
    Cotangent cot Θ adjopp cosΘsinΘ = 1tanΘ

Vectors

  • A vector is an array of numbers representing a magnitude (length) and direction in a coordinate space, but without a specific position in space.
  • Vectors are shown in brackets, and may be represented horizontally (a row vector) or vertically (a column vector).
  • Each number in a vector represents a displacement along an axis (basis vector) of the coordinate space it is used in. A vector is a combination of all its displacements. For example, vector [x y] represents a displacement x units along the x-axis, and y units along the y-axis.
  • For any given vector dimension there exists a special vector called the zero vector, for which each dimension's displacement is zero. It represents the concept of "zero displacement."
  • Every vector v has an additive inverse -v of the same dimension as v such that v + (-v) = 0. To derive the inverse of a vector, negate each component of the vector, producing a vector of equal magnitude but opposite direction.

Magnitude / Length

  • The magnitude of a vector is signed ||v||.
  • The magnitude of a vector of arbitrary dimension is calculated by: v = ∑ i = 1 n v i 2
  • Translated: the square root of the sum of the squares of each element.

Addition and Subtraction

  • Vectors may be added or subtracted if they are of equal dimension; addition and subtraction are undefined for vectors of unequal dimension.
  • Each element of the sum vector is made by adding the equivalent elements of the two input vectors: a b c + x y z = a+x b+y c+z
  • Subtraction is performed by inverting the second vector before adding it.
  • The 'triangle rule' of vector addition states that starting at a point and applying the displacements of two vectors produces the same result as applying the combination of those two vectors. Visually plotting a, b, and a+b will produce a triangle, unless a == -b or a ∥ b.
  • Vector addition is commutative, but vector subtraction is not.

Multiplication and Division by a Scalar

  • Multiplying a vector by a scalar produces a vector.
  • Each element of the product is formed by multiplying the scalar value by the equivalent element of the input vector: k x y z = kx ky kz
  • Division may be performed by multiplying the vector by the reciprocal of the scalar.
  • Multiplication of a vector by a scalar is commutative: kv = vk
  • Geometrically the effect of multiplying a vector by a scalar is to increase or decrease the magnitude of the vector.

Normalization

  • A normalized vector has magnitude 1 and is symbolized v^ .
  • For any nonzero vector v, the normalized vector v^ is arrived at by dividing v by its magnitude: v^ = v v = v ∑ i = 1 n v i 2

Dot Product / Inner Product

  • There are two methods of multiplying vectors--the dot/inner product (which produces a scalar) and the cross product (which produces a vector).
  • The dot product is the sum of the products of corresponding components, and is only defined for vectors of equal dimension.
  • Example: a b c ⋅ x y z = ax+ by+ cz
  • There are some important ways to visualize the dot product:
    • As a representation of one vector's length when projected onto the axis of another vector. The product a⋅b is equal to the signed length of the projection of b onto any line parallel to a, multiplied by the length of a. Since the product is a signed scalar, a negative value for a⋅b indicates b points in the opposite direction from a. If a⋅b has zero length, a and b are perpendicular.
    • As a representation of the angle between two vectors, via cosine. Posit that a and b are unit vectors. Therefore, a right triangle can be formed with sides b and a⋅b, where b is the hypoteneuse, a⋅b is the adjacent side, and Θ is the angle between them. Therefore the dot product becomes equivalent to cos Θ: adjhyp = a^ ⋅ b^ 1 = a^ ⋅ b^ = cosΘ More generally, you can say that the dot product of two vectors (normal or otherwise) is equal to the cosine of the angle Θ between them, multiplied by the length of the vectors: a⋅b = ‖a‖ ‖b‖ cos Θ. Solving for Θ gives us an easy way to compute the angle between the two vectors: Θ= arccos a⋅b ‖a‖ ‖b‖
  • Projection of a vector onto an axis gives you the displacement on that axis alone, so if you project onto a cardinal axis you get one part of the coordinates that the vector displaces to. Projecting onto a vector x that lies on the x-axis, for instance, gives the units of x that the vector head end displaces to.
  • By projecting b onto a, you can separate b into two parts--the part parallel to a and the part perpendicular to a.
  • Properties of the dot product:
    • The dot product is commutative: a ⋅ b = b ⋅ a
    • The dot product is associative with multiplication by a scalar. a ⋅ (kb) = k(a ⋅ b)
    • The dot product is distributive over addition and subtraction: a ⋅ (b + c) = a ⋅ b + a ⋅ c

Tensor / Dyadic Product

The tensor product is a special case of the dot product, in which a column vector is multiplied by a row vector to produce a full sized matrix:

a ⋅ bT = a1 ⋮ am ⋅ b1 … bn = cij = aij bij i=1 , j=1 m,n

Cross Product

  • The cross product can only be defined in 3 dimensions, and is symbolized a ⊗ b.
  • The equation for the cross product is: a b c ⊗ x y z = bz-cy cx-az ay-bx
  • The cross product is not commutative--it is actually anti-commutative: a ⊗ b = -(b ⊗ a)
  • The cross product is not associative, and in general (a ⊗ b) ⊗ c != a ⊗ (b ⊗ c)
  • Geometrically the cross product yields a vector which is perpendicular to the original two vectors, which are (because any two vectors are) co-planar.
  • The length of the cross product vector is equal to the magnitudes of a and b and the sine of the angle between them: ‖ a ⊗ b ‖ = ‖a‖ ‖b‖ sin Θ
  • The cross product is also the area of the parallelogram formed starting with sides a and b and angle Θ
  • If the vectors are parallel, or either is the zero vector, the cross product is the zero vector. The zero vector is parallel to all other vectors under the cross product, in contrast to the dot product which considers it perpendicular to all other vectors.
  • There are two possible directions for a ⊗ b to point given that it is perpendicular to a plane. To determine the direction of the cross product, place the tail of b at the head of a, and determine whether you make a clockwise or counterclockwise turn. In a left handed system, a ⊗ b points towards you on a clockwise turn; right handed systems are the opposite.

Coordinate Spaces / Basis Vectors

  • A coordinate space is defined by axes, fundamental vectors which establish cardinal directions and units, and an origin, where those vectors intersect.
  • The position of a coordinate space is defined by the position of its origin relative to any parent coordinate space. Relative to itself, a local coordinate space always has an origin of (0, 0, [0, ..., 0]).
  • Axes (basis vectors) are typically unit vectors.
  • Any vector in a dimensional space can be decomposed into a sequence of axially aligned displacements, as coefficients of the basis vectors for that space: v = x p + y q + z r
  • In orthonormal 3D space, the basis vectors are: p = 100 q = 010 r = 001

Matrices

  • A matrix is typically a representation of the coefficients for a system of linear equations, arranged in a grid.
  • Linear systems may be represented in the form Ax = B, where A is a matrix of coefficients, x is a column vector of unknowns, and B is a column vector of constants.
  • The following representations are equivalent:
    • Algebraic form: 3x+y-2z=8 -x+2y+3z=6 4x+3y-z=2
    • Matrix form, row picture: 3 1 -2 -1 2 3 4 3 -1 x y z = 8 6 2
    • Matrix form, column picture: x 3 -1 4 + y 1 2 3 + z -2 3 -1 = 8 6 2
  • A matrix is described as being an "m by n matrix," where m is the number of rows and n is the number of columns.
  • Individual elements of matrix A are addressed at aij, where i is the row number and j is the column number.

Matrix Types

  • Square matrix - where m = n
  • Column matrix - where n = 1
  • Row matrix - where m = 1
  • Null / Zero matrix - where all elements = 0, denoted 0
  • Identity matrix - denoted I, a square matrix where all elements i=j are 1 and all elements i!=j are 0.
  • Lower triangular matrix - denoted L, a matrix where all elements i<j are 0.
  • Upper triangular matrix - denoted U, a matrix where all elements i>j are 0.
  • Transpose matrix - for matrix A, denoted AT. If B = AT, then all elements bij = aji.
  • Symmetric matrix - when AT = A.
  • Skew symmetric / antisymmetric matrix - when AT = -A.

Matrix Equality

Matrix A is equal to matrix B if and only if each element of A is equal to the equivalent element of B:

A mn = B mn iff aij = bij for i ∈ 1 m ∧ j ∈ 1 n

Addition and Subtraction

  • Addition and subtraction are only defined for matrices of the same dimensions.
  • For A ± B = C, each element cij = aij ± bij
  • Example: a11 a12 a13 a21 a22 a23 a31 a32 a33 ± b11 b12 b13 b21 b22 b23 b31 b32 b33 = a11±b11 a12±b12 a13±b13 a21±b21 a22±b22 a23±b23 a31±b31 a32±b32 a33±b33

Multiplication and Division by a Scalar

The product of multiplying a matrix by a scalar is a matrix of the same dimension, with each element individually multiplied by the scalar:

B mn = s A mn ⇒ bij = aij ⋅ s for i ∈ 1 m ∧ j ∈ 1 n

Division is done by multiplication with the inverse of the scalar.

Multiplication of Matrices

  • AB is defined for those matrix pairs where A is m by n and B is n by p.
  • The dimensions of the matrix product of AB will be m by p.
  • Each element cij is the sum of the products of each pair aik * bkj for k=1 through k=n: c ij = ∑ k=1 n a ik b kj
  • Each element in cij can also be phrased as the dot product of row i of A and column j of B.
  • Each column of C is a combination of the columns of A. Each column of B produces a column in C which is the combination of the columns of A.
  • Each row of C is a combination of the rows of B for the same reasons.
  • AB also equals the sum of the columns of A times the rows of B, since each column by row multiplication is a full size matrix, and you can add full size matrices of the same size.
  • It is possible to section matrices into blocks, and do block row times block column multiplication, then recombine them to form the output matrix.

Point in Sphere

A sphere may be considered to be a point with a radius. For a sphere S of radius R, and a point P, P is inside S if and only if the distance from P to the center of S is less than R:

(Px-Sx)² + (Py-Sy)² + (Pz-Sz)² < R ²
